Q1Force Direction on Proton (RHR 2)
A moving charge in a magnetic field gets shoved sideways — always sideways, never speeding up or slowing down. The direction of the shove depends on which way the charge moves and which way the field points. Your right hand is the tool that figures it out.
a crosswind hitting a jogger — the wind pushes you sideways but doesn't slow you down or speed you up.
1. Charge moves through B field
→ B pushes charge perpendicular to both v and B
→ RHR 2: fingers along v, curl toward B, thumb = F direction
→ For negative charge: flip F direction
→ If v parallel to B: sinθ = 0, so F = 0
MATH CONFIRMS
F = qvB sinθ
Force equals charge times speed times field strength times how perpendicular they are
METHOD
Use Right-Hand Rule #2: Point fingers in direction of v, curl toward B. Thumb = F direction (for + charge).
(a) v → (right), B ↑ (up) ⇒ F out of page (⊙)
(b) v ↑ (up), B ⊗ (into page) ⇒ F right (→)
(c) v ⊙ (out of page), B → (right) ⇒ F up (↑)
MNEMONIC: "FBI" — F = Thumb, B = Curl-to, I(v) = Fingers point
For NEGATIVE charges (electrons), flip the force direction!
Source: notes-19 p1, notes-20 p2
RHR 2 applied to three cases: v right/B up, v up/B into page, v out of page/B right
Connects to: Q2 — reverse problem Q4 — what force does over time
▶ Variations 6
V1: Electron instead of proton
→ Same RHR 2 setup, then flip F direction for negative charge
KEY F points opposite to what RHR gives for + charge
V2: v parallel to B (F = 0 trap)
→ sin(0°) = 0, so F = qvB×0 = 0 regardless of q, v, or B magnitude
Common exam trap: F = 0 when v and B point same direction!
V3: v at angle θ to B
→ F = qvB sinθ — only the perpendicular component of v contributes
F = qvB sinθ
V4: Find v given F, q, B
v = F / (qB sinθ)
Rearrange F = qvB sinθ for v
V5: 3D direction combos
→ Systematically apply RHR 2 for each axis combination: +x/+y, +x/+z, +y/+z, etc.
V6: Find θ given F, q, v, B
sinθ = F / (qvB)  →  θ = arcsin(F / qvB)
Inverse-sine of the force ratio gives the angle between v and B
Q2Find B from Known v and F (RHR 2 Reverse)
You know something is pushing a charge, and you know which way it's moving — now figure out which direction the invisible field must be pointing. It's like detective work: the clues are the motion and the push, and the field is the culprit you're tracking down.
figuring out which direction the wind is blowing by watching which way a flag leans.
1. Know F direction (the observed push)
→ Know v direction (how the charge is moving)
→ RHR 2 in reverse: palm faces F, fingers along v
→ The direction fingers curl toward = B direction
→ Negative charge? Flip B direction at the end
MATH CONFIRMS
B = F / (qv sinθ)
Field strength equals force divided by charge-times-speed (times sine of angle)
CONCEPT
Given v and F directions, find B by reversing RHR 2. Point fingers along v, thumb along F — palm pushes toward B.
Ex 1: v → (right), F ↑ (up) ⇒ B out of page (⊙)
Ex 2: v → (right), F ⊗ (into page) ⇒ B down (↓)
KEY
For electrons: apply RHR as if positive, then flip B direction.
Common mistake: forgetting to flip for negative charges!
Source: notes-19 p1, notes-20 p1
Reverse RHR 2: given v right and F direction, deduce B field direction
Connects to: Q1 — forward problem
▶ Variations 5
V1: Electron (negative charge)
→ Apply RHR 2 as if positive charge, then flip the B direction you find
Always flip B (not F) when working backwards for a negative charge
V2: Wire current form (F = BIL)
B = F / (IL sinθ)
Same logic: divide force by current times length — works for a current-carrying wire
V3: All 3D combos table
→ Systematically tabulate: given F in +x and v in +y → B in −z, etc.
V4: Find B magnitude only
|B| = |F| / (|q||v| sinθ)
When you only need the size, not the direction — plug in and divide
V5: Is B unique? (given F magnitude only)
→ No — many B directions can produce the same F magnitude if the angle θ compensates
Direction is uniquely determined only when you know both F direction AND v direction!
Q3Forces Between Parallel Wires
Two wires carrying current create magnetic fields that reach each other. Those fields push on the other wire's current. Same direction currents snuggle together. Opposite direction currents shove apart.
two people walking the same direction on a narrow sidewalk — they drift together. Walking opposite directions? They dodge apart.
1. Wire 1 carries I₁ → creates B field circling around it (RHR 1)
→ Wire 2 sits inside that B field
→ Wire 2's current feels a force: F = BIL (current in a field)
→ Same direction currents: force points toward wire 1 = ATTRACT
→ Opposite direction currents: force points away from wire 1 = REPEL
MATH CONFIRMS
F/ℓ = μ₀I₁I₂ / (2πR₁₂)
Force per length equals permeability times both currents divided by (2π times the distance between wires)
RULE
Same direction currents → ATTRACT
Opposite direction currents → REPEL
WORKED EXAMPLE
I₁ = I₂ = 25 A, R = 35 cm = 0.35 m, ℓ = 15 cm = 0.15 m
F/ℓ = (4π×10⁻⁷)(25)(25) / (2π)(0.35) = 3.57×10⁻⁴ N/m
F = (3.57×10⁻⁴)(0.15) ≈ 5.4×10⁻⁴ N
MNEMONIC: "Same = Snuggle, Opposite = Oh no!" (attract vs repel)
Source: notes-19 pp2-4
Left: same-direction currents attract. Right: opposite-direction currents repel.
Connects to: Q1 — force on a wire
▶ Variations 5
V1: Different I magnitudes (I₁ ≠ I₂)
→ Plug both values into F/ℓ = μ₀I₁I₂/(2πR) — forces on each wire are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction (Newton 3)
V2: Find I given F/ℓ and R
I = √(2πR · F/ℓ / μ₀)
When both currents are equal, rearrange and take the square root
V3: Three parallel wires
→ Net force on middle wire = sum of forces from wire 1 and wire 3 (direction depends on each pair's current directions)
V4: Force per unit length only
F/ℓ = μ₀I₁I₂ / (2πR₁₂)
When ℓ isn't given, just report force per meter — no extra step needed
V5: B at midpoint between wires
→ Same direction: B fields cancel at midpoint (B = 0). Opposite direction: B fields add at midpoint.
B = 0 between same-direction parallel wires is a classic exam question!
Q4+ Charge in B Out of Page → Clockwise Circle
A positive charge enters a magnetic field and gets constantly shoved sideways. Since the shove is always perpendicular to motion, the charge curves — and keeps curving — tracing a perfect circle at constant speed. The field steers without ever pushing forward or back.
a car on an icy road turning the steering wheel — the tires push sideways, changing direction without changing speed.
1. Charge enters B field (B out of page, charge moving right)
→ Force is perpendicular to v: RHR 2 gives F downward
→ Charge curves downward
→ As direction changes, force rotates too (always stays ⊥ to new v)
→ Continuous perpendicular force = perfect circle
→ + charge in B⊙ moving right: F down → clockwise (CW) path
MATH CONFIRMS
R = mv / (qB)
Radius equals momentum (mass times speed) divided by how strongly the field grabs the charge
DIRECTION
+ charge enters B⊙ (out of page) moving right → RHR gives F downward → curves down → Clockwise circle
ANSWER
Clockwise circular path
Negative charge in same setup → COUNTERCLOCKWISE
Source: notes-19 p5, notes-20 p2
+ charge enters B (out of page) moving right — force pushes down, tracing a clockwise circle
Connects to: Q1 — the sideways force
▶ Variations 6
V1: Negative charge (CCW)
→ Same B (out of page), same entry direction — but force flips for negative charge → counterclockwise path
KEY Negative charge → opposite rotation direction
V2: Find period T and frequency f — TRAP
T = 2πm / (qB)    f = qB / (2πm)
Period is independent of v and R — faster charge just makes a bigger circle in the same time!
Classic exam trap: T does NOT depend on speed or radius!
V3: B into page instead of out
→ B into page + + charge moving right: RHR gives F upward → counterclockwise path
V4: Helix (v at angle to B)
→ Component of v parallel to B is unaffected (no force) → charge spirals along B direction in a helix
V5: B doubles → R halves
R = mv/(qB)  →  R ∝ 1/B
Stronger field = tighter circle. Double B, radius cuts in half at the same speed.
V6: Both v and B double (R unchanged)
→ R = mv/(qB) → if v doubles AND B doubles: numerator ×2, denominator ×2 → R unchanged
Another exam trap: proportional changes in v and B cancel out in R = mv/(qB)!
Q5Variable Resistor → Induced Current in Loop A
You slide a resistor dial and that changes the current in a circuit. The changing current changes the magnetic field. A nearby loop "notices" the changing field and fights back by creating its own current.
a thermostat — when the room gets warmer, it kicks on the AC to push back. Nature resists change.
1. Slider moves → R changes
→ I changes (V = IR, V fixed)
→ B changes (B ∝ I)
→ Φ through loop A changes
→ Lenz's Law: loop creates current to oppose the change
MATH CONFIRMS
ε = N|ΔΦ/Δt|
EMF equals number of turns times how fast the flux is changing
ANSWER
(b) Counterclockwise in loop A
MNEMONIC: Lenz = "Nature is Lazy" — opposes all change
Source: notes-22 pp2-3, notes-21 pp3-4
Slider decreases R → I rises → B rises → loop A fights back with CCW current
Connects to: Q7 — trap: steady vs changing Q8 — moving magnet version Q16 — rod version
▶ Variations 6
V1: Slider moves opposite direction (R increases)
→ R increases → I decreases → B decreases → Φ decreases
→ Lenz opposes decrease → loop induces current to maintain flux
KEY Current direction flips to CW — always oppose the change
V2: Switch opens or closes instead of slider moving
→ Opening switch: I drops to zero instantly → B drops → Φ drops → Lenz induces CW current
→ Closing switch: I rises from zero → B rises → Lenz induces CCW current
V3: Loop moves closer or farther away
→ Closer: more flux through loop (field stronger nearby) → Lenz opposes increase
→ Farther: less flux → Lenz opposes decrease
V4: Loop rotates relative to field
→ Φ = BA cosθ — as θ changes, flux changes → EMF induced
ε = NBAω sin(ωt)
This is the generator equation — rotating a loop in B produces sinusoidal AC
V5: Battery removed suddenly
→ I drops to zero → B collapses → Φ through loop drops → Lenz induces current to oppose the loss
Same physics as opening the switch — sudden change, Lenz fights back
V6: What if there are two nearby loops?
→ Both loops independently "see" the changing flux and each induces its own opposing current
→ Mutual inductance: each loop's induced current also creates a field that the other loop notices
KEY Transformer principle — two coils coupled by mutual inductance
Q6KE of Particle in Circular Path
The magnetic force only pushes sideways — it never pushes along the direction of motion. Since it never pushes "with" or "against" the charge, it does zero work. Zero work means zero change in kinetic energy.
a Ferris wheel — it constantly redirects your seat sideways, but you're not going faster at the top vs bottom. The motor changes your direction, not your speed.
1. F is always ⊥ to v (perpendicular, by the nature of the cross product)
→ Work = F · d · cos(90°) = 0
→ W = 0 means ΔKE = 0
→ Speed is constant — only direction changes
MATH CONFIRMS
W = Fd cos90° = 0     ΔKE = W = 0
Work equals force times distance times the cosine of the angle between them. At 90°, cosine is zero.
ANSWER
(c) Remains constant — F ⊥ v means W = 0 means ΔKE = 0
B field changes DIRECTION of velocity, never SPEED!
Source: notes-19 p5, notes-20 pp2-3
F is always centripetal (toward center), v is always tangential — they are always 90° apart
Connects to: Q4 — the circular path Q9 — speed changes = radius changes
▶ Variations 4
V1: Does velocity change? (Yes — direction!)
→ Speed is constant, but direction continuously changes
→ Velocity is a vector — same magnitude, new direction = different velocity
KEY Speed unchanged, velocity changed — these are not the same thing!
V2: Does momentum change? (Yes — direction!)
→ p = mv — magnitude constant, but direction rotates continuously
→ Momentum is a vector, so it changes even though |p| is constant
V3: Is acceleration zero? (NO — this is THE trap)
→ Centripetal acceleration exists: a = v²/R, pointing inward
→ Acceleration means changing velocity direction — not changing speed
Classic trap: "no work" does NOT mean "no acceleration." Centripetal acceleration is always present!
V4: What if an electric field E is added?
→ E field force (qE) CAN be parallel to v → does work → KE changes
→ Path is no longer a pure circle — becomes a spiral or cycloid
KEY Add E field and now KE does change — the B-field-only rule breaks down
Q7Steady 1.5A in Solenoid → Loop Current
A solenoid has a constant, unchanging current flowing through it. That creates a constant magnetic field. A nearby loop sits in that steady field and feels... nothing. No change = no induction. This is the #1 Faraday trap.
standing in a room with the lights on — you don't notice the light until someone flickers it. Steady state is invisible to induction.
1. Current is steady → B is steady
→ Φ is steady
→ dΦ/dt = 0
→ ε = 0 → No induced current
MATH CONFIRMS
ε = −dΦ/dt = 0  (when Φ is constant)
The rate of change of flux is zero because nothing is changing
ANSWER
ZERO — a steady current produces no change in flux
This is the #1 Faraday trap! "Steady" = "constant" = NO induction. Only CHANGING currents induce.
Source: notes-22 p2
Steady 1.5A → constant B → constant flux → zero EMF → zero induced current
Connects to: Q5 — changing current = induction (contrast!) Q8 — changing Φ from motion
▶ Variations 4
V1: Current is increasing (now there IS induction)
→ Rising I → rising B → rising Φ → dΦ/dt > 0 → ε ≠ 0
→ Lenz: loop opposes increase → induced current creates opposing B
KEY Change is the trigger — not the presence of a field
V2: Current is decreasing
→ Falling I → falling B → falling Φ → Lenz opposes decrease
→ Induced current in loop tries to maintain the field — opposite direction to V1
V3: Current oscillating (AC source)
→ AC current: I = I₀ sin(ωt) → B oscillates → Φ oscillates → EMF oscillates
ε = −N dΦ/dt = −NBAω cos(ωt)
Fastest change happens when sine is steepest (at zero crossing) — that's when EMF peaks
V4: What if the loop moves toward the solenoid?
→ Even with steady I in solenoid, loop moving closer captures more flux
→ Φ changes because loop position changes → EMF induced
Trap: steady solenoid current + moving loop STILL causes induction — motion changes Φ!
Q8Bar Magnet Through Loop
A magnet falls toward a loop. As it approaches, the flux through the loop grows — and the loop fights back by creating a current that repels the magnet. Once the magnet passes through and moves away, flux decreases — and the loop switches its current to attract the magnet, still fighting the change.
a bouncer at a door — pushes you away when you're coming in, grabs you when you're leaving. Always opposes your motion.
Phase 1: Magnet approaches → Φ↑ → Lenz opposes increase → loop repels → CCW
Phase 2: Magnet leaves → Φ↓ → Lenz opposes decrease → loop attracts → CW
MATH CONFIRMS
ε = N|ΔΦ/Δt|    direction from Lenz's Law
EMF magnitude from Faraday, direction from Lenz
ANSWER
Current REVERSES direction as magnet passes through. Approaching: CCW. Leaving: CW.
MNEMONIC: "Coming in? Push away. Going out? Pull back." — Lenz always fights change.
Source: notes-21 p3, notes-22 p2
Left: magnet approaching, loop repels (CCW). Right: magnet leaving, loop attracts (CW).
Connects to: Q5 — different cause, same Lenz Q7 — if magnet stops → no current Q16 — rod version
▶ Variations 6
V1: S-pole approaching instead of N-pole
→ Field lines point upward into loop (toward S) instead of downward
→ Flux still increases as it approaches → Lenz still opposes → current direction flips vs N-pole case
KEY Same Lenz logic, opposite field direction → CW current (approaching S-pole)
V2: Magnet stationary, loop moves toward magnet
→ Relative motion is what matters — loop moving toward a stationary magnet = same result
→ Flux through loop increases → Lenz opposes → same induced current as V1
It's relative motion that drives flux change — either object moving gives the same physics!
V3: Horizontal setup (magnet moves sideways through loop)
→ Apply the same two-phase logic: approaching side → Φ rises, leaving side → Φ falls
→ Direction of induced current determined by which pole faces loop and which way flux changes
V4: At exact center — momentary zero current
→ At exact midpoint of the loop, flux is momentarily at its maximum
→ dΦ/dt = 0 at the peak → EMF = 0 → current = 0 at that instant
Trap: maximum flux does NOT mean maximum current — it means ZERO current (rate of change is zero)!
V5: Two coils (transformer preview)
→ Moving magnet through primary coil → changing flux in secondary coil
→ Same Lenz/Faraday physics — this is the operating principle of a transformer
V₂/V₁ = N₂/N₁
V6: What if the loop has resistance R?
→ EMF is set by Faraday: ε = N|ΔΦ/Δt|
→ Induced current follows Ohm's Law: I = ε/R
I = N|ΔΦ/Δt| / R
Higher resistance → same EMF, smaller current — Lenz force on magnet is weaker
Q94× Speed → 4R Radius
A faster charge has more momentum, so the magnetic field has a harder time bending its path. Double the speed, double the radius. Quadruple the speed, quadruple the radius.
driving a car around a curve — the faster you go, the wider you swing out. Same road, but speed determines how tight the turn.
1. v increases → mv increases
→ Field can't bend it as sharply
→ R = mv/(qB)
→ R scales linearly with v
MATH CONFIRMS
R = mv/(qB)    R ∝ v
Radius is directly proportional to speed
ANSWER
New radius = 4R (radius scales linearly with speed)
MNEMONIC: "Faster = wider arc" — R proportional to v, NOT v² (that's KE).
Source: notes-19 p5, notes-20 p3
Same B field, quadruple speed → quadruple radius. Linear relationship.
Connects to: Q4 — base formula Q6 — KE relationship Q15 — alpha particle
▶ Variations 6
V1: Speed halved
→ v → v/2 → mv halves → R = mv/(qB) halves
KEY R halves. Direct proportion — always the same ratio.
V2: Mass doubled
→ m → 2m → numerator mv doubles → R doubles
→ Heavier particle is harder to bend — same logic as faster particle
V3: B field doubled
→ B → 2B → denominator qB doubles → R halves
→ Stronger field bends the path more tightly
V4: Charge doubled
→ q → 2q → denominator qB doubles → R halves
→ Higher charge → stronger force → tighter curve
V5: Speed AND B both doubled
→ v → 2v and B → 2B → numerator doubles, denominator doubles
→ They cancel — R is UNCHANGED!
Trap: doubling both speed and field leaves radius the same. Changes in numerator and denominator cancel.
V6: Find period T (biggest trap)
→ T = circumference / speed = 2πR / v = 2π(mv/qB) / v
T = 2πm/(qB) — INDEPENDENT of v!
Period does NOT depend on speed. Faster particle, bigger circle, same time around. This is the cyclotron principle.

A battery powers a solenoid, turning it into an electromagnet. A bar magnet sits nearby. The solenoid has its own N and S poles. Like poles repel, unlike attract.
two refrigerator magnets — flip one around and it switches from sticking to pushing away.

→ RHR 1 determines which end is N
→ Compare solenoid's pole with bar magnet's facing pole
→ Like poles repel, unlike attract
→ Force direction follows
MATH CONFIRMS
Bsolenoid = μ₀nI = μ₀(N/L)I
Solenoid field equals permeability times turns per length times current
ANSWER
Unlike poles face each other → ATTRACT (pull toward each other)
MNEMONIC: RHR 1 — "Curl fingers with current, thumb points to N pole"

Solenoid N pole faces bar magnet S pole → unlike poles attract.

▶ Variations 5
V1: Reverse battery polarity
→ Current reverses → RHR 1 gives opposite N/S assignment
→ Poles flip → force direction reverses (attract ↔ repel)
KEY Reverse the battery, reverse the force. The solenoid is just an electromagnet you can flip.
V2: Bar magnet flipped around
→ Now the N end of bar magnet faces solenoid N pole → like poles → REPEL
→ Same trick as flipping a fridge magnet — switches from stick to push

Q13R and 4R in SERIES — P(4R)=40W → P(R)=?
Two resistors in series share the same current. Since power equals I²R, the bigger resistor dissipates more power. If 4R produces 40W, R produces exactly 1/4 of that — 10W.
two water wheels on the same stream — the bigger wheel catches more energy. Same flow rate (current), but the bigger wheel (bigger R) extracts more.
1. Series → same I through both resistors
→ P = I²R, so P ∝ R when I is fixed
→ P(4R) = I²(4R) = 40W
→ I²R = 40W ÷ 4 = 10W
→ P(R) = 10W
MATH CONFIRMS
P = I²R    P ∝ R (in series)
Power is proportional to resistance when current is the same
ANSWER
P(R) = 10W — in series, larger R gets MORE power
KEY RULE: SERIES: same I → use P = I²R → larger R gets MORE power
Source: notes-16 p3
Same current I through both resistors → bigger R dissipates more power (P = I²R)
Connects to: Q14 — parallel (opposite rule) Q12 — switch changes config
▶ Variations 5
V1: Three resistors in series — find total power
→ Each has same I → Ptotal = I²R⊂1; + I²R⊂2; + I²R⊂3; = I²(R⊂1;+R⊂2;+R⊂3;)
→ Total power = I² × Rseries
KEY Sum the individual power terms, or use Ptotal = I²Rtotal.
V2: R and 4R in series — find the current
→ P(4R) = I²(4R) = 40W → I² = 10/R
→ Need battery voltage or R value to get a numerical answer for I
Trap: you need one extra piece of info (V or R) to find I alone.
V3: R and 2R in series (exam: 20W in 2R → P(R)=?)
→ P ∝ R → P(R) = P(2R)/2 = 20/2 = 10W
KEY Scale by resistance ratio.
V4: R and 3R in series (exam: 30W in R → P(3R)=?)
→ P ∝ R → P(3R) = 3 × P(R) = 3 × 30 = 90W
KEY Scale by the resistance ratio in the direction that makes sense: bigger R, more power.
V5: Which resistor is hotter in a string of lights (series)?

CH 19 — Circuit Laws

Ohm's Law
The current through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage across it and inversely proportional to its resistance. V = IR. Only applies to ohmic materials (constant R). A resistor's R does not depend on V or I.
Kirchhoff's Junction Rule (KCL)
At any junction in a circuit, the total current entering equals the total current leaving. ΣI_in = ΣI_out. Based on conservation of charge — charge cannot accumulate at or vanish from a junction.
Kirchhoff's Loop Rule (KVL)
Around any closed loop in a circuit, the sum of all voltage gains and drops is zero. ΣV = 0. Based on conservation of energy — a charge returning to its starting point must have the same potential energy.
Joule's Law (Resistive Heating)
Current through a resistor converts electrical energy irreversibly into thermal energy (heat). P = I²R = V²/R = IV. The energy is lost and cannot be stored — unlike a capacitor which stores energy in its electric field.
Series vs. Parallel Principle
Series: Same current through all elements, voltages add. Adding R increases total resistance. Parallel: Same voltage across all elements, currents add. Adding R decreases total resistance (more paths for current).

CH 20 — Magnetism Laws

Lorentz Force Law
A charged particle moving through a magnetic field experiences a force perpendicular to both its velocity and the field. F = qv × B. The force is maximum when v ⊥ B and zero when v ∥ B. Because F ⊥ v always, the magnetic force does no work and cannot change a particle's speed — only its direction.
Right-Hand Rule (Principle)
For force: Point fingers along v, curl toward B, thumb points in F direction (for + charge). For negative charges, flip F. For wire's B field: Thumb along current I, fingers curl in B direction. For solenoid: Curl fingers with current, thumb = N pole.
Biot-Savart Law
Every small segment of current-carrying wire contributes to the magnetic field. For a long straight wire: B = μ₀I/(2πr). B decreases as 1/r from the wire. Field lines form concentric circles around the wire.
Ampère's Law
The line integral of B around any closed loop equals μ₀ times the total current enclosed. Used to derive B inside a solenoid: B = μ₀nI (uniform inside, ≈0 outside). Also gives the field of straight wires and toroids.
Superposition Principle
The net magnetic field at any point is the vector sum of fields from all sources. This is how we find where B = 0 between parallel wires, or add B fields from a wire and a loop. Always add as vectors (direction matters!).
Gauss's Law for Magnetism
There are no magnetic monopoles. Every magnetic field line that enters a closed surface must also exit it. The net magnetic flux through any closed surface is zero: ∮B·dA = 0. This is why magnets always have both N and S poles.
Force Between Parallel Currents
Same direction currents attract. Opposite direction currents repel. Why? Each wire sits in the other's B field, and F = IlB gives a force toward or away. F/l = μ₀I₁I₂/(2πd). This defines the SI ampere.

CH 21 — Induction Laws

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction
A changing magnetic flux through a circuit induces an EMF (voltage). ε = −N(dΦ/dt). The EMF depends on the rate of change of flux, not the flux itself. Flux can change by changing B, A, or the angle φ between B and the area normal. A constant flux produces zero EMF regardless of how large B is.
Lenz's Law
The induced current flows in a direction that opposes the change in flux that caused it. If flux is increasing, induced current creates opposing B. If flux is decreasing, induced current supports the existing B. This is a consequence of conservation of energy — if it didn't oppose, you'd get perpetual motion. The minus sign in Faraday's law encodes Lenz's law.
Motional EMF
A conductor moving through a magnetic field develops a voltage across it. ε = vBL (when v, B, L mutually ⊥). The magnetic force pushes charges to one end, creating a potential difference. If v is parallel to B or parallel to L, no EMF is induced.
Magnetic Flux
Φ = BA cosφ where φ is the angle between B and the area normal (NOT the surface). Φ is maximum when B ⊥ surface (φ = 0°), and zero when B ∥ surface (φ = 90°). Units: Weber (Wb) = T·m².
Transformer Principle
AC in a primary coil creates a changing B that induces EMF in a secondary coil. V₂/V₁ = N₂/N₁. Power is conserved: V₁I₁ = V₂I₂. Step-up (N₂ > N₁) increases voltage but decreases current. Does NOT work with DC — requires changing flux.
Self-Induction & Back-EMF
A changing current in a coil induces an EMF in itself that opposes the change (Lenz's law applied to its own flux). ε_L = −L(dI/dt). An inductor resists changes in current. When a switch is opened, the rapid dI/dt can produce a large voltage spike. Energy stored: E = ½LI².

Foundational Principles

Conservation of Energy
Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. In circuits: battery chemical energy → electrical → heat (resistors) or stored (capacitors/inductors). This principle underlies Kirchhoff's loop rule, Lenz's law, and why magnetic force does no work (it only redirects, never creates or destroys KE).
Conservation of Charge
Electric charge can neither be created nor destroyed. The total charge in a closed system is constant. This underlies Kirchhoff's junction rule and why current is the same through all elements in series.
Principle of Superposition
The net electric or magnetic field at any point is the vector sum of all individual fields. This allows us to analyze complex circuits (superposition theorem) and find B from multiple wires by adding each wire's contribution as vectors.
Work-Energy Theorem
The net work done on a particle equals its change in kinetic energy: W_net = ΔKE. Since magnetic force is always ⊥ to velocity, it does zero work → KE is unchanged → speed is constant in a magnetic field. Only direction changes (circular orbit).
Newton's Second Law (applied to charged particles)
F = ma. For circular motion in B: qvB = mv²/R → R = mv/(qB). The magnetic force provides centripetal acceleration. Larger mass or speed → larger radius. Stronger B → tighter circle.

→ Proton: q = e, m = mp → rp = mpv / (eB)
→ Alpha: rα = 2mpv / (eB) = 2rp
KEY Alpha particle circles at twice the radius of a proton at the same speed — mass wins over charge.
V2: What if you need to find B given the radius?
→ Rearrange r = mv/(qB): B = mv / (qr)
→ Plug in alpha values: B = (4mp)v / (2e × r)
KEY Same formula, rearranged. Always identify q and m for the specific particle first.
E1 Metal Loop Pushed Into Uniform B Field FROM 2010 EXAM
A rectangular loop gets pushed into a region where a magnetic field exists. As the leading edge enters, flux starts changing, and Lenz's Law determines which end of the resistor is at higher potential.
pushing a sponge into a stream — the water creates a pressure difference across the sponge as it enters.
1. Loop pushed rightward into B field region
→ Leading edge enters B; trailing edge still outside
→ Area inside B increases → Φ increases (B into page)
→ Lenz opposes increase → induced B must point out of page
→ Current flows CCW (by RHR) → determines which side of R is higher potential
MATH CONFIRMS
ε = BLv
EMF equals field times edge length times entry speed
ANSWER
End "b" of resistor R is at higher potential (current flows from b through external circuit).
Source: 2010 Exam Q8
Loop pushed right into B (into page). Flux ↑ → Lenz induces CCW current → end b at higher potential.
Connects to: Q8 — both involve changing flux + Lenz Q16 — same motional EMF formula ε=BLv
▶ Variations 3
V1: Loop exiting the B field (pulled rightward out)
→ Area inside B decreases → Φ decreases → Lenz opposes decrease
→ Induced current reverses direction (CW) → end a is now at higher potential
KEY When exiting, current reverses — Lenz always fights the change.
V2: Loop fully inside the B field (TRAP)
→ Entire loop inside uniform B → area in B is constant → Φ = constant
→ dΦ/dt = 0 → ε = 0 → no induced current
TRAP: Being inside a B field does NOT cause induction — only CHANGING flux does!
V3: Loop enters from top edge instead of right side
→ Top edge enters B first → same flux increase logic applies
→ Use ε = BLv with L = width of loop, v = downward entry speed
KEY Same formula, same Lenz logic — just identify which edge is the "cutting" edge.
E2 Magnetic Flux at Different Angles FROM 2010 EXAM
A circle lies flat in the xz plane. The normal to this circle points straight up (y-direction). Magnetic flux measures how much field "threads through" the circle — it depends on the angle between B and the normal.
holding a hula hoop in the rain. Rain falling straight through the hoop = maximum flux. Rain falling sideways along the hoop's plane = zero flux. At an angle, it's cos(θ) of the maximum.
Setup: Circle in xz plane → normal = ŷ (points up)
→ Φ = BA cosθ where θ = angle between B and normal
Case a: B along z → θ = 90° → cos90° = 0 → Φ = 0
Case b: B at 60° from ŷ → Φ = BA cos60° = BA/2
Case c: B along ŷ → θ = 0° → cos0° = 1 → Φ = BA (maximum)
MATH CONFIRMS
Φ = BA cosθ    A = πr²
Flux = field × area × cosine of angle between field and surface normal
WORKED EXAMPLE
r = 6 cm = 0.06 m, B = 0.23 T, A = π(0.06)² ≈ 0.01131 m²
(a) B along z: Φ = 0
(b) B at 60° from ŷ: Φ = 0.23 × 0.01131 × cos60° ≈ 1.30 × 10−3 Wb
(c) B along ŷ: Φ = 0.23 × 0.01131 ≈ 2.60 × 10−3 Wb
KEY INSIGHT: Φ = 0 when B is parallel to the surface (perpendicular to normal). Φ = maximum when B is perpendicular to the surface (parallel to normal).
Source: 2010 Exam Q16
Circle in xz plane, normal = ŷ. B along z → zero flux. B along y → max flux.
Connects to: Q5 — flux feeds into Faraday's Law Q8 — changing flux drives induction
▶ Variations 4
V1: Circle in xy plane (normal = &zcirc;)
→ Normal points along z → θ measured from z-axis
→ B along z: θ=0° → Φ=BA. B along x: θ=90° → Φ=0
KEY Always identify the normal first, then find angle between B and that normal.
V2: Circle in yz plane (normal = x̂)
→ Normal points along x → B along x gives maximum flux
→ B along y or z: each is perpendicular to normal → θ=90° → Φ=0
V3: B at arbitrary angle θ to normal
→ Φ = BA cosθ — general formula always applies
→ If given angle from surface (not from normal): use sin instead of cos
TRAP: Angle from surface and angle from normal are complementary — cos(θnormal) = sin(θsurface)
V4: Square loop instead of circular loop
→ Same formula: Φ = BA cosθ where A = L² for a square of side L
→ Shape doesn't matter for flux — only area and angle matter
KEY Flux formula is shape-independent. Only total area and orientation determine Φ.
E3 Finding B = 0: Opposite-Direction Wires FROM 2010 EXAM
Two wires carry currents in opposite directions. Unlike same-direction wires (where fields cancel between them), opposite-direction wires have fields that ADD between them and cancel OUTSIDE. The zero point is outside, near the weaker wire.
two speakers playing out of phase — between them the sounds reinforce, but outside on one side they cancel. The quiet spot is closer to the quieter speaker.
1. Opposite currents → fields ADD between wires (no cancellation there)
→ Fields oppose each other OUTSIDE the wire pair
→ Zero point exists outside, on the side of the weaker wire
→ Set B₁ = B₂: μ₀I₁/(2πr₁) = μ₀I₂/(2πr₂) → I₁/r₁ = I₂/r₂
→ Solve for position x from weaker wire (I₁ = 25 A): 25/x = 75/(x+0.4)
MATH CONFIRMS
B = μ₀I / (2πr)    Set B₁ = B₂
Find distance where both fields are equal and opposite → net B = 0
WORKED EXAMPLE
I₁ = 25 A, I₂ = 75 A, separation d = 40 cm = 0.4 m
25/x = 75/(x + 0.4) → 25(x + 0.4) = 75x → 25x + 10 = 75x → x = 0.2 m
Zero point is 0.2 m from the 25 A wire, on the OPPOSITE side from the 75 A wire.
Source: 2010 Exam Q18
Opposite currents: B adds between wires, cancels outside near weaker wire. Zero at 0.2 m left of I₁.
Connects to: L5 — same-direction version (zero between wires)
▶ Variations 3
V1: Same-direction currents (zero between wires instead)
→ Same-direction: fields ADD outside, CANCEL between wires
→ Zero point is BETWEEN the wires, closer to the weaker current
→ Set I₁/x = I₂/(d−x) → solve for x between 0 and d
Opposite vs Same direction is the key switch: zero point jumps from OUTSIDE to BETWEEN!
V2: Three wires with mixed directions
→ Add field contributions (with signs) at each point: Bnet = B₁ + B₂ + B₃
→ Use RHR for each wire's field direction at the point of interest
KEY Superposition — each wire contributes independently. Add as vectors, not scalars.
V3: Find B at a specific point between the wires
→ Compute B₁ = μ₀I₁/(2πr₁) and B₂ = μ₀I₂/(2πr₂) at that point
→ Opposite directions → fields point same way between wires → Bnet = B₁ + B₂
Bnet = (μ₀/2π)(I₁/r₁ + I₂/r₂) between opposite-current wires
